**Sonnet 4.5**

- **Density (72)**: The screenplay maintains strong narrative focus throughout most sections, particularly in the Dawn of Man and Discovery sequences. However, there's notable bloat in technical procedures (docking sequences, passport automation, vision phone calls) and some repetitive dialogue establishing Hal's malfunction. The extensive narration in Part IV, while thematically rich, borders on over-explanation.
- **Payoff (88)**: The screenplay demonstrates exceptional structural discipline with its setups and payoffs across a 4-million-year narrative span. Nearly every significant element introduced—from the bone-weapon to Hal's lying programming—receives proper resolution. The few elements that don't pay off (like some technical details) are appropriately atmospheric rather than structural failures.
- **Key Setups**: Moonwatcher learning to use tools/weapons (pays off in defeating Big-Tooth and the Others), the mysterious cube teaching violence (pays off throughout human evolution), Hal's truth programming conflict (established in crew conversation, pays off in malfunction), the TMA-1 signal to Saturn (pays off in final destination), separate crew training (pays off when revealing hidden mission purpose), Floyd's recorded briefing (planted early, delivered late), flip-bolts being over-tightened (setup for Poole's death scene)
- **Balance (9)**: The heavily visual, direction-dominant approach is perfectly suited to Kubrick's cinematic vision and the story's scope. The sparse dialogue emphasizes isolation and the inadequacy of human communication against cosmic mysteries. The ratio shifts appropriately—more visual in Part I (Dawn of Man) and Part IV (Star Gate), more dialogue-heavy in the Hal malfunction sequences where verbal communication becomes the dramatic focus.
- **Craft**: This is masterclass screenplay architecture that prioritizes visual storytelling and thematic resonance over conventional dialogue-driven drama. The structure boldly spans epochs with minimal exposition, trusting the audience to connect dots across massive time jumps. Technical sequences are perhaps over-detailed for a shooting script, suggesting Kubrick's meticulous pre-visualization process. The Hal sequences demonstrate sophisticated dramatic escalation through increasingly tense verbal exchanges. The screenplay's greatest strength is its confidence in silence and image—entire sequences (Dawn of Man, space travel montages) convey complex ideas without dialogue. The weakness lies in occasional over-explanation through narration, particularly in Part IV, where showing would serve better than telling. Overall, it's a structurally ambitious work that earns its epic scope through disciplined setup/payoff mechanics and thematic unity across disparate time periods.
